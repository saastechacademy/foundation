# Popular Omnichannel Configurations

# Routing \+ Ship From Store

Omnichannel fulfillment is not just about shipping faster. Routing and Ship From Store should protect store operations, reduce order rejections and balance online demand with walk-in traffic.

Common concerns when enabling Routing \+ Ship from Store

1. ## Protect in-store customers while selling online

   **Set safety stock at stores**  
   Reserve a portion of store inventory for walk-in customers so online orders don’t empty store shelves.  
     
   Apply higher inventory buffers to high-velocity SKUs to reduce stockouts during peak demand.  
     
   **Fulfill new launches from warehouses first**  
   Route new, limited-edition, high-demand product launches to warehouses initially and involve stores only after demand stabilizes.  
     
   **Prioritize locations with broken styles**  
   Route online orders to stores with limited sizes or variants so stores with full assortments stay available for walk-ins.

2. ## Handle product-specific fulfillment needs

   **Route based on product**  
   Ship products with custom fulfillment steps like engraving or alterations from locations that support them.

3. ## Reduce markdowns

   **Ship slow-moving inventory first**  
   Prioritize stores where inventory has been sitting longer to reduce markdowns.  
   

4. ## Prevent stores from being overwhelmed by online orders

   **Use stores selectively for fulfillment**  
   Enable ship from store only for locations with available space, staffing and fulfillment capacity.  
     
   **Apply order limits per store**  
   Set daily order caps to prevent store teams from being overloaded during peak periods.  
     
   **Apply lower routing priority for high-traffic stores**  
   Deprioritize stores with heavy walk-in traffic, such as mall locations, to protect the in-store experience.  
     
   **Make stores offline on demand**  
   Remove stores from fulfillment during staff shortages, emergencies or local rush periods.  
   

5. ## Handle high-priority orders and tighter SLAs

   **Route based on order priority and SLA**  
   Assign high-priority orders to closest locations that can meet tighter SLAs at lower shipping costs.  
     
   **Prioritize fulfillment of urgent orders in-store**  
   Give stores an option to sort their fulfillment queue by ship method.  
   

6. ## Reduce order splits

   **Limit splits to orders that require them**  
   Allow order splitting for high-priority orders where meeting the promised SLA matters more than shipping together.  
     
   **Avoid splitting low-value orders**  
   Force low-value orders into single shipments using shipment value thresholds.  
     
   **Prevent splits for kits and gift with purchase**  
   Fulfill kits and gift with purchase items from a single location so items arrive together as intended.  
   

7. ## Reduce order rejections and overselling

   **Global inventory thresholds**  
   Apply a global inventory buffer to prevent overselling across channels.  
     
   **Fulfill from stores with sufficient inventory**  
   Prefer stores with higher inventory confidence using brokering safety stocks. Only fallback to stores with low inventory if no other location can fulfill the item.  
   

8. ## Handle exceptions

   **Allow partial rejection**  
   Reject only unavailable items of an order and fulfill the remaining items to prevent delivery delays.  
     
   **Apply collateral rejection**  
   Automatically reject unavailable items from all open orders at a store so they can be rerouted to locations that can fulfill them.  
     
   **Auto-reroute on store rejection**  
   Automatically reroute orders to the next best location when a store rejects an order.  
     
   **Use fallback fulfillment locations**  
   Route orders to predefined fallback locations that do not participate in online fulfillment but can be used as a last resort when inventory is unavailable anywhere else.
